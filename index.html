About me

I am a fourth year PhD student in Economics at the University of Cambridge and a Cusanuswerk, Cambridge Trust, and Kurt Hahn Trust scholar. Currently, I am visiting the University of Vienna.

I am passionate about Behavioral Political Economy and Experimental Economics. Specifically, I am interested in the effect of (political) information and institutional environments on how individuals perceive, trust, and cooperate with each other.

Before starting my PhD, I worked as Pre-Doctoral Fellow at the Hub for Equal Representation at the LSE. I received my MSc in Economics at the London School of Economics and completed my BSc in Economics at the University of Mannheim.

Work in Progress

  • More Bark Than Bite? Affective Polarization Around The 2024 U.S. Presidential Election

    with Toke Aidt and Edoardo Gallo.

    (Click to view abstract)

    We show that the 2024 U.S. Presidential election increased hostile behavior among rivaling partisans. The standard survey metric for partisan animosity fails to capture this effect. Conducting a three-wave online experiment around the election, we compare behavior in incentivized trust and cooperation games with survey metrics of polarization including the canonical feeling thermometer. In swing states, behavioral polarization increases sharply around the election, while the polarization measured by the feeling thermometer remains unchanged. The effect is persistent ten weeks after the election. We find no change in affective polarization for participants outside of swing states. Democrats show higher hostility than Republicans across all metrics, yet changes in behavior are uncorrelated with stated animosity, underscoring the divergence between metrics. Our findings demonstrate that polarization is multidimensional and reveal previously neglected social cost of elections, namely increased hostility among voters.

  • Tying Yourself to The Mast - Informational Regimes and Public Good Provision

    with Edoardo Gallo.

    (Click to view abstract)

    Asymmetric power over resource allocation and information is common in various environments. We study how unilateral control over information shapes cooperation when one actor can appropriate common resources. We experimentally vary the transparency of actions in a repeated public good game in which one group member -- the Leader -- can embezzle from others' contributions. In a between subject design we determine if participants' actions are transparent, opaque, or if the Leader can manipulate the information. In a final treatment, we allow the Leader to endogenously choose among these informational regimes. When the flow of information is determined exogenously, contributions are highest when the Leader can manipulate information even though others know that these messages may be misleading. Contributions are lowest when all actions are fully transparent. The pattern reverses when informational regimes are endogenous to the Leader's selection. Groups achieve the highest welfare when the Leader voluntarily commits to full transparency. Our results show that transparency fosters cooperation only when it is self-imposed rather than enforced, providing experimental evidence for the behavioral foundations of voluntary accountability and informational control in organizations and politics.
